Welcome! I am **Mingtao Zhou**, a Ph.D. candidate in Finance. My research interests lie at the intersection of **climate finance**, **asset pricing**, **financial forecasting**, and **empirical asset allocation**.

My current research focuses on how climate-related risks shape the dynamics of financial asset prices. In particular, I study whether climate risk contains useful predictive information for agricultural commodity returns, global stock market volatility, and other financial market outcomes. I am also interested in the economic value of climate risk information for investors, especially through out-of-sample forecasting and dynamic portfolio allocation.

Research Agenda
======

A central theme of my research is the predictive role of climate risk in financial markets. I examine whether climate risk indicators improve forecasts of asset returns and market volatility, how this predictive power varies across time and regions, and through which economic channels climate risk affects asset price dynamics.

My work pays particular attention to the distinction between **physical climate risk** and **transition climate risk**. I explore whether these two dimensions contain different information for financial markets and whether physical climate risk plays a more stable and informative role in predicting asset price movements.

Selected Research Topics
======

**Climate Risk and Agricultural Commodity Returns**  
This research investigates whether aggregate climate risk helps predict agricultural commodity returns and whether such forecasts generate economic value for investors.

**Climate Risk and Global Stock Market Volatility**  
This research examines the time-series predictive power of climate risk for stock market volatility across international markets, with emphasis on regional heterogeneity and out-of-sample forecasting performance.

**Physical versus Transition Climate Risks**  
This research studies the asymmetric predictive effects of physical and transition climate risks on financial markets, highlighting the distinct information content of different climate risk dimensions.

**Economic Anxiety, Investor Attention, and Market Volatility**  
I also study how public attention to economic conditions, measured using Google Trends and related textual data, helps forecast international stock market volatility.

Methods
======

My empirical work uses a range of econometric and machine learning methods, including predictive regressions, principal component analysis, forecast combination, random forests, neural networks, and out-of-sample forecast evaluation. I am particularly interested in connecting statistical predictability with economic value through portfolio allocation and utility-based performance measures.
